## SLIDE 1: COVER — "Half a Good House"

**[~30 seconds — let the image breathe, then introduce]**

> This is Quinta Monroy. What you're looking at is three half-houses — delivered exactly like this. Concrete structure, timber staircases, open voids where walls should be. This is what $7,500 of government money bought in Iquique, Chile in 2004.
>
> Our case study is about why this was the right answer to the wrong question — and how one architecture firm redesigned not a house, but an entire system.

---

## SLIDE 2: THE PROBLEM — Narrative overlay

**[~90 seconds — this is the stage-setting. Slow down. Let the numbers land.]**

> Before we can understand what Elemental did, we need to understand what Chile was already doing — because Chile wasn't failing. Chile was the *model*.
>
> In the early 2000s, Chile was building a hundred thousand social housing units a year. They'd delivered 1.7 million homes in three decades. International development agencies pointed to Chile as proof that market-based housing subsidies work. On paper, the slums were disappearing.
>
> But here's what those homes actually looked like: $7,500 per family — and that has to cover land, infrastructure, and the house itself. So the government buys the cheapest land it can find — always on the urban periphery — and builds a 36-square-meter box. A bathroom, a kitchen, a room, done. Complete. Final. No room to grow.
>
> The academics who studied the long-term outcomes coined a devastating phrase: *"Los con techo"* — "Those with a roof." Twenty years ago the problem was families without a roof. Now the problem *is* the families with one. The houses themselves became poverty traps.

---

## SLIDE 3: CONTEXT (Q1) — Chile's broken system + Iquique

**[~90 seconds — shift from macro to micro. Bring it to the specific site.]**

> So what made these houses into traps? It's a feedback loop. Properties on the periphery depreciated — they lost value instead of gaining it. Mortgage delinquency hit 70%. Families were commuting two to three hours each way to reach jobs in the city center. Social networks — the mutual aid, the informal economy, the childcare arrangements that kept these communities alive — all shattered on relocation.
>
> Now zoom in. Iquique — a port city in northern Chile, edge of the Atacama Desert. In the center of the city, on a half-hectare lot called Quinta Monroy, roughly a hundred families had been living since the 1960s. Thirty-plus years. Informal, illegal, no formal infrastructure. 60% of rooms had no natural light. A fire in 1980 left twenty families homeless.
>
> Then in 2000, the landowner dies. The land triples in value. The families face eviction. The government's default move: relocate them to the periphery. But these families organized. They refused to leave. And the government — which had a national commitment to eliminate informal settlements — couldn't ignore them.

---

## SLIDE 4: FEEDBACK LOOP

**[~45 seconds — walk through the loop step by step, pointing at each element]**

> This is the system Elemental identified. It's a reinforcing feedback loop.
>
> Start here: government buys cheap peripheral land. Families are isolated from jobs and services. Property values stagnate or decline. Families can't build equity. They stay poor. And because they stay poor, any future housing move leads back to cheap peripheral land. The cycle repeats.
>
> This is the critical insight: this is not a *housing* problem. It's a *location* problem that manifests as housing. And Elemental broke it at the root — the land decision. They kept the families on central land, even though it cost three times the standard allocation.

**[~20 seconds — let the image speak. It's beautiful.]**

> This is what Elemental delivered. Look at this space. Concrete walls, a timber staircase, a shaft of light. This is the "void" — the structural skeleton of a house. Everything a family cannot build themselves is here. Everything they *can* build is missing. On purpose.

## SLIDE 6: DESIGN (Q2) — Half a Good House

**[~90 seconds — explain the reframe. This is the intellectual core.]**

> So here's the reframe that made the whole project possible. With $7,500 and central land costing three times the budget, Elemental couldn't build a complete house. The standard response is: move them to cheaper land. Aravena asked a different question: "What if 36 square meters is not a small house, but *half* of a good one?"
>
> On the left — the before and after. Top: the interior as delivered. Bare concrete, orange panels, a sink. Bottom: the same unit years later, fully furnished, a family living in it.
>
> Elemental built the "difficult half" — the reinforced concrete frame, the foundation, the roof, the kitchen, the bathroom, the staircase, all the infrastructure. They left the other half as a structural void. They called it "middle-class DNA" — housing designed to grow into a 72-square-meter middle-class home, not shrink into a depreciating box.
>
> Two typologies: 34 ground-floor units expandable to 70 square meters, and 59 upper-floor duplexes expandable to 72. All arranged around four courtyards designed to preserve the neighbor relationships from the original settlement.

## SLIDE 10: SYSTEMS (Q3) — Beyond HCD

**[~90 seconds — this is the answer to the course's central question]**

> So why does this move beyond user-centered design? Because a user-centered designer would have made a *better* 36-square-meter house. Better layout. Better kitchen. Better materials. That's optimizing the product.
>
> Aravena didn't optimize the product. He changed four systems simultaneously.
>
> First: government policy. He convinced the housing ministry to let the subsidy buy half a unit instead of a whole one — something that had never been done.
>
> Second: land economics. He spent 70% of the budget on *location* instead of construction. Most social housing does the opposite.
>
> Third: construction logic. He designed for the 72-square-meter end state, not the 36-square-meter delivery. The structure anticipates the finished house.
>
> Fourth: the role of the resident. Not a passive recipient of a finished product, but a co-builder and investor. Sweat equity becomes financial equity.
>
> Aravena said something in his TED talk that captures this perfectly: "What we're trying to do by asking people to participate is envision what is the *question*, not what is the answer. There's nothing worse than answering the wrong question well."

---

## SLIDE 11: PARTICIPATORY DESIGN

**[~60 seconds — the hunger strike is the hook]**

> The participatory process had a pivotal moment. When Elemental proposed apartment blocks — which would fit more families on the expensive central land — the residents threatened a hunger strike. They refused any design that couldn't expand. Apartments are finished products. You can't add a room. You can't build a rental unit on top.
>
> That rejection *was* the design innovation. The constraint produced the half-house concept. And the key question they asked residents: "If you can only have half a house now, which half do you need us to build?"
>
> This connects directly to Costanza-Chock's Design Justice framework: "Nothing about us without us." The hunger strike wasn't an obstacle — it was the most important data point in the entire project.

---

## SLIDE 12: QUINTA MONROY — Before/after image

**[~45 seconds — the question on screen is rhetorical. Answer it.]**

> What happens when you give 93 families half a house on the most expensive land in the city?
>
> Within one year, property values tripled — from $7,500 to over $20,000. Within twelve years, 92 of 93 families had expanded their homes. Not just adding rooms — some built rental apartments for income. Some started businesses on the ground floor. One resident kept her unit specifically because of its proximity to the city's main vegetable market, even after she moved away.
>
> The house became an investment vehicle. Not a depreciating box on the periphery, but an appreciating asset in the center of a functioning city.

---

## SLIDE 13: IMPACT (Q4) — Mostly yes, but

**[~45 seconds — be honest here. The professor wants critical thinking, not hagiography.]**

> Does moving beyond human-centered design make it better? The honest answer is: mostly yes, but with real problems.
>
> The positive feedback loop reversed. Central location leads to economic access, which leads to home expansion, which increases value, which builds wealth, which enables further investment. The house became what Aravena calls "a tool to overcome poverty" rather than merely "a shelter against the environment."
>
> But — and this matters for the next slide — the model isn't without flaws. A strong analysis acknowledges both.

---

## SLIDE 14: VILLA VERDE — Earthquake narrative

**[~60 seconds — shift to the scaling story. The earthquake grounds it.]**

> February 27, 2010. An 8.8-magnitude earthquake hits Chile — one of the six most powerful ever recorded. Constitución, a city of 46,000, is devastated. Tsunami waves reach 15 meters. 61% of the city floods. 80% of buildings are ruined.
>
> Elemental is hired — funded by the forestry company ARAUCO, with engineering support from Arup — to create not just housing, but a complete city master plan. 484 incremental houses. A 16-hectare forest designed to absorb future tsunami energy. Public space tripled.
>
> This is the scaling proof. Quinta Monroy was 93 families on half a hectare. Constitución was a city-level master plan for 46,000 people. Same participatory method. Same incremental logic. The model worked beyond its original context.

## SLIDE 15: TIMELINE

**[~30 seconds — scan the key moments, emphasize the bookends]**

> The timeline spans two decades. Quinta Monroy completed in 2004. Venice Biennale recognition in 2008. The earthquake and Constitución rebuild in 2010-2013. The Pritzker Prize in 2016 — the first Chilean architect to win architecture's highest honor. Aravena open-sourced the designs: four housing projects, downloadable for free. 40,000 downloads in the first year.
>
> And then 2025. Aravena's own assessment: "It's still a failure. The mainstream has not been affected." That self-awareness is as important as the award.

---

## SLIDE 16: CRITICISM

**[~75 seconds — this is where you show the professor you can think critically. Don't rush it.]**

> A strong case study doesn't just celebrate. It interrogates. Four substantive criticisms.
>
> First: the construction skill gap. Not every family can build. Twelve years later, researchers found that many additions used "low-durable and poor quality materials similar to the slum-like conditions seen in the previous informal settlement." 31% of units encroached on public courtyards. The freedom to build, without support, can reproduce the very conditions the design was meant to replace.
>
> Second: the ideological critique. Boano and Vergara Perucich asked the sharpest question: "Why should the poor receive a half-house instead of a proper one?" Their argument: the model normalizes inadequate state provision. It shifts the construction burden onto the poorest families and calls it empowerment.
>
> Third: participation ended at handover. The researchers found that "the participative processes claimed by Elemental ended when residents received their homes." Individual agency without collective governance. No ongoing technical support. No community management structure.
>
> Fourth: scalability. Despite the Pritzker, despite the open-source plans, despite global media — the dominant housing model worldwide hasn't changed. Aravena himself says so.

---

## SLIDE 17: PEOPLE + CHANGE (Q5 + Q6)

**[~60 seconds — land the conclusion. This is where you tie it together.]**

> Who was affected? Concentrically: 93 families directly. Government officials who had to approve something unprecedented. 2,500 families in subsequent projects. 40,000 people who downloaded the plans. And the architecture profession itself — the Pritzker Prize shifted the conversation about what architecture should be *for*.
>
> Did it create meaningful change? At the project level: yes. Families kept their land, built equity, stayed connected to opportunity. At the city level: yes. Constitución proves the model scales. At the global level: not yet. The mainstream hasn't moved.
>
> And that's the conclusion that makes this a *systems* case study rather than just a design case study. The system bent. It didn't break. The meaningful change is real — but bounded. The tension between proof-of-concept and systemic transformation is the story.

## Q&A PREP — Anticipated Questions

**"Why not just give them more money?"**
> The Lincoln Institute found that subsidy increases tend to get absorbed by land price inflation — the market eats the difference. Doubling the subsidy doesn't double the house; it doubles the land cost. Aravena's argument is pragmatic: within the *existing* budget reality, half a good house produces better outcomes than a whole bad one. The critics' argument is ideological: the budget reality itself is a political choice.

**"Isn't this just gentrification?"**
> Property values tripled, which raises the question. The evidence so far: families stayed. But one analysis notes they may be "locked in by lack of alternatives rather than truly choosing to stay." Van Noorloos et al. identify a structural tendency — as incremental neighborhoods consolidate, land values rise, and displacement pressure follows. It hasn't happened yet at Quinta Monroy, but the structural risk is real.

**"How does this connect to Design Justice?"**
> Costanza-Chock's framework says design should be led by marginalized communities and should challenge structural inequalities, not reproduce them. Quinta Monroy does both — the hunger strike gave residents real design authority, and the location decision challenged the structural inequality of peripheral relocation. But the critique applies too: participation ended at handover, and the architects retained authority over fundamental decisions. It's a partial implementation of design justice, not a complete one.

**"Could this work in the US / another country?"**
> Aravena himself is skeptical. Chile has a unique housing subsidy system, a political culture that treats housing as a right (forged in the 1960s-70s land seizures), and a construction culture that supports self-building. Ann Varley at UCL warned that without those conditions, the model becomes "a hollow aesthetic showcase." The open-source plans are necessary but not sufficient — the institutional and political context matters as much as the architectural design.

**"What's the difference between this and Habitat for Humanity?"**
> Habitat builds complete houses with volunteer labor. Elemental builds incomplete houses with professional construction and leaves expansion to residents. The key difference is *what's designed for*: Habitat designs for the delivery moment. Elemental designs for the *trajectory* — the 72 sqm end state, not the 36 sqm handover. And the location decision — central vs. peripheral — is a systems intervention that Habitat's model typically doesn't address.
