Elemental / Quinta Monroy — A Systems Case Study

Ian Arnoldy + Yuchen Zhang

IXDS 709 — Systems Innovation: Beyond Human-Centered Design — SCAD — Spring 2026

The Problem

In the early 2000s, Chile was building 100,000 social housing units per year. It was the model for the developing world.

The government subsidy of $7,500 per family bought a 36 m² box on the cheapest land available — always on the urban periphery, far from jobs, schools, and hospitals. Commutes stretched to two or three hours. Properties depreciated. Mortgage delinquency hit 70%. Social networks shattered on relocation.

Nearly 1.7 million homes were delivered in three decades. The academics called it “Los con techo” — “Those with a roof.” The housing problem was no longer families without a roof. It was the families with one. The houses themselves had become poverty traps.

01 / Context

Q1 — What was the context?

Chile delivered 1.7 million homes in three decades. The houses trapped families in poverty.

The government subsidy of $7,500 per family produced 36 m² boxes on cheap peripheral land — far from jobs, schools, and hospitals. Properties depreciated. Mortgage delinquency hit 70%. Commutes stretched to 2–3 hours. Social networks shattered on relocation. The academics called it “Los con techo” — “Those with a roof.”

In the center of Iquique — a port city on Chile’s northern desert coast — roughly 100 families had been living on a half-hectare lot called Quinta Monroy since the 1960s. 60% of rooms lacked natural light. A fire in 1980 left 20 families homeless. When the landowner died in 2000, the land tripled in value and the families faced eviction. The families organized and refused.

The Displacement Feedback Loop
Cheap peripheral land → Isolation from jobs → Property depreciates → No equity → Trapped in poverty → Can only afford cheap land → Cycle repeats

Elemental broke this loop at the root: the land decision. They kept families on central land — even though it cost three times the standard allocation.

02 / Design

The Void

The structural skeleton delivered by Elemental — everything a family cannot build themselves. The other half waits.

Q2 — How did context affect its design?

Half a Good House

With $7,500 and land costing three times the standard allocation, Elemental couldn’t build a complete house. Aravena reframed: “What if 36 m² is not a small house, but half of a good one?”

They built the “difficult half” — concrete frame, roof, kitchen, bathroom, staircase, all infrastructure. The other half: a structural void. They called it “middle-class DNA” — housing designed to grow into 72 m².

03 / Systems

Beyond HCD

Four systems redesigned. Not one interface improved.

Q3 — Why does this move beyond user-centered design?

A user-centered designer would have made a better 36 m² house. Aravena changed the system.

Government Policy

Changed what the subsidy was allowed to buy — half a unit instead of a whole one. Convinced MINVU through the VSDsD program.

Land Economics

Spent 70% of the budget on central location instead of construction. Location is the intervention.

Construction Logic

Designed for the 72 m² end state, not the 36 m² delivery. Incomplete but expandable beats complete but fixed.

Role of the Resident

Co-builder and investor, not passive recipient. 92 of 93 families expanded within 12 years.

“What we’re trying to do by asking people to participate is envision what is the question, not what is the answer. There’s nothing worse than answering the wrong question well.”

— Alejandro Aravena, TED Talk (2014)
Participatory Design
01

The Hunger Strike

Residents threatened a hunger strike when apartments were proposed. They rejected any design that couldn’t expand. This constraint produced the half-house innovation.

02

The Right Question

“If you can only have half a house now, which half do you need us to build?” Residents chose: stay on this land, ability to expand, ground-level access, middle-class standard.

03

Design Justice

Connects to Costanza-Chock’s framework: “Nothing about us without us.” The hunger strike was not an obstacle — it was the most important data point.

Iquique, Chile — 2004

Quinta Monroy

What happens when you give 93 families half a house on the most expensive land in the city?

Elemental spent 70% of the budget keeping families on central land. Within one year, property values tripled. Within twelve years, 92 of 93 households expanded. The house became an investment vehicle, not a depreciating box.

04 / Impact

Q4 — Does beyond-HCD make it better?

Mostly yes. But with real problems.

92 of 93 families expanded their homes. Property values increased from $7,500 to over $20,000 within a year. Central location was preserved. Housing extensions became income sources — rental units, businesses. The house became a wealth-building platform.

The positive feedback loop reversed: central location → economic access → home expansion → increased value → household wealth → further investment. Aravena: “a tool to overcome poverty” rather than merely “a shelter against the environment.”

But: construction quality is uneven — additions often use low-quality materials. 31% of units encroached on courtyards. Participation ended at handover. The model hasn’t scaled globally. Aravena: “still a failure.”

Constitución, Chile — 2013

Villa Verde

On February 27, 2010, an 8.8-magnitude earthquake struck Chile — one of the six most powerful ever recorded.

Constitución was devastated. 61% flooded, 80% of buildings ruined. Elemental created a city master plan: 484 incremental houses, a 16-hectare tsunami buffer forest, public space tripled. Quinta Monroy was 93 families. Constitución was 46,000 people. The model scaled.

05 / Criticism

Q4 — Honest analysis

The system bent. It didn’t break.

Construction Skill Gap

Not all families can build. Many additions use “low-durable and poor quality materials” (O’Brien & Carrasco, 2021). 31% of units encroached on public courtyards.

“Why Should the Poor Get Half a House?”

Boano & Vergara Perucich (2016) argue the model normalizes inadequate state provision — shifting the construction burden onto the poorest families.

Participation Ended at Handover

Carrasco & O’Brien (2021): “the participative processes claimed by Elemental ended when residents received their homes.” Individual agency without collective governance.

Scalability

Despite the Pritzker Prize and 40,000 plan downloads, the dominant housing model worldwide remains unchanged. Aravena (2025): “It’s still a failure.”

06 / People

Q5 — What groups did this affect?

93 families — housed on their own land, given a wealth-building platform

Government officials — who approved an unconventional half-house model

2,500+ families — in subsequent Elemental projects across Chile and Mexico

40,000 downloaders — of the open-source plans worldwide

The architecture profession — the Pritzker shifted what design is for

07 / Change

Q6 — Did it create meaningful change?

Project level: Yes. 92/93 expanded. Equity built. Economic connection preserved.

City level: Yes. Constitución proves scaling — 93 families to 46,000 people.

Global level: Not yet. The mainstream has not been affected.

The meaningful change is real but bounded. The system bent. It didn’t break. That tension is what makes this a systems case study.
